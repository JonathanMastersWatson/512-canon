### What the Kernel Is

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL

The kernel is seven sentences. It fits in 512 bytes.
It can be read (by a human) in under a minute. It can be audited
in its entirety by any competent engineer in an
afternoon. It does not require interpretation. It
does not require a committee to apply it. It requires
only that a system either satisfies it or does not.

This is not modesty. It is a design requirement.
A governance mechanism that cannot be evaluated at
machine speed cannot govern machine-speed systems.
The kernel is small because small is the only size
that works at the execution boundary.

### The Seven Invariants

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL

No agent may initiate force or fraud against any human.
All interactions must be voluntary and based on explicit consent.
Consent may be withdrawn. Exit must always be possible.
All contracts must be explicit, readable, and equally
enforceable by all parties.
No rules governing interaction may be hidden or
unilaterally changed.
On failure, systems must fail open, reveal governing
rules, and default to human choice.
The kernel is immutable. Adherence is binary.

---

### Why These Seven

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL · EXECUTION-BOUNDARY

These seven invariants were not designed. They were
identified. They represent the minimum conditions
that any system governing voluntary interaction must
satisfy — conditions that have governed legitimate
human systems for centuries and that must now be
enforced mechanically because humans can no longer
enforce them at execution speed.

Remove any one and a structural gap opens. The first
invariant removed creates an attack surface for
force or fraud. Remove the second and consent becomes
optional. Remove the third and exit becomes impossible.
Remove the fourth and contracts become unenforceable
by one party. Remove the fifth and rules can be hidden.
Remove the sixth and failure becomes coercive. Remove
the seventh and the kernel itself becomes negotiable.

Seven is not an aesthetic choice. Seven is the
discovered minimum.

### What the Kernel Does Not Do

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL

The kernel does not tell systems what to do. It tells
systems what they must not do — and what they must
do on failure. Everything else is free.

This is the distinction between a constraint and
a prescription. A constraint limits the space of
possible actions. A prescription defines which action
to take. 512 is purely constraint. It places no
positive obligations on systems beyond the six
structural requirements and the fail-open requirement.

This means 512 is compatible with almost any
implementation. It does not prescribe technology,
architecture, language, or domain. Any system that
satisfies all seven invariants at every execution
boundary exhibits 512's properties. Any system that
does not, does not.
