### The Broadcast Problem

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

Broadcast and digital media operate at near-zero
downtime tolerance. Content decisions — what airs,
when, under what editorial constraints — are made
continuously, by combinations of human editors and
automated systems, across multi-vendor workflows
where no single system has visibility into the
complete decision chain.

When something goes wrong — incorrect content
broadcast, regulatory violation, rights infringement,
editorial policy breach — the question is always
the same: how did this happen, who authorized it,
and what was the system state at the moment the
decision was made?

Today, answering these questions requires assembling
fragments from multiple systems, each with its own
logs, timestamps, and event models. Each system knows
its own part of the story. No system can reconstruct
the complete causal narrative — who decided what,
in what sequence, under what declared policy, at
what exact moment.

CVS fills that gap.

### Why Broadcast Is the Wedge Market

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · GEOMETRIC-DISJOINT

Broadcast is where sidecar necessity becomes obvious
because it has all four conditions that make CVS
immediately valuable:

**Near-zero downtime tolerance** — the sidecar must
be fail-open by construction. Any evidence system
that could interrupt broadcast is not deployable.
CVS's fail-open design makes it the only viable
architecture for this environment.

**Multi-vendor, human and machine workflows** — no
single vendor controls the complete workflow. NLEs,
newsroom systems, MAMs, graphics, automation, and
distribution are all separate systems. The sidecar
must observe across all of them without being embedded
in any of them.

**High legal, reputational, and regulatory exposure** —
broadcast organizations face regulatory scrutiny,
rights disputes, and reputational risk from content
decisions. The cost of being unable to prove what
happened is measured in regulatory fines, litigation
settlements, and audience trust.

**No end-to-end system of record** — when something
goes wrong today, each tool knows its own logs.
No system can reconstruct the full causal narrative.
CVS is that system of record.

### What CVS Provides to Broadcast

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

**Causal narrative reconstruction**
When an incident occurs, CVS provides the complete
sequence of decisions — human and automated — that
led to the outcome. Not fragments from individual
systems. A single, cryptographically verified timeline
covering the complete workflow.

**Regulatory compliance proof**
Regulators require broadcasters to demonstrate
compliance with content standards, rights agreements,
and editorial policies. CVS provides this proof
at execution time — not assembled retroactively
from imperfect records.

**Rights and licensing evidence**
Content rights disputes require proof of what was
broadcast, when, and under what licensing authority.
CVS records this at the moment of broadcast.
The record cannot be altered. It does not depend
on the cooperation of any party to the dispute.

**Editorial accountability**
When automated systems make editorial decisions —
content selection, scheduling, routing — CVS records
what the system decided, under what declared policy,
at what moment. Human editors can verify that
automated decisions stayed within declared parameters.

### The Installation Model

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · GEOMETRIC-DISJOINT

In broadcast environments, the sidecar installation
model is critical. Primary systems must not be
modified. Workflows must not be interrupted.

The sidecar taps into observability infrastructure
that broadcast systems already expose:
- Mirror ports and SPAN/TAP connections
- Multicast subscriptions
- Read-only APIs
- File system observers
- Event listeners

These are sensors, not participants. They observe
without touching. The primary systems — NLEs,
newsroom systems, MAMs — continue operating exactly
as before. The sidecar adds one more subscriber
to existing event infrastructure.

This is geometric disjoint in practice. The evidence
plane observes the execution plane without entering
it. No workflow is modified. No system is touched.
No downtime is introduced.
