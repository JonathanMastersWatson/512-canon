### The Problem of Over-Exposure

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: DISCLOSURE-KERNEL · CVS-SIDECAR

Complete transparency creates as many problems as
opacity. An organization required to expose its
entire execution record to prove one claim exposes
far more than the claim requires — proprietary
business logic, counterparty relationships, internal
processes, and sensitive operational data that have
nothing to do with the claim being verified.

This is the over-exposure problem. It creates a
dilemma: prove the claim and expose everything, or
protect the sensitive data and leave the claim
unproven.

The Disclosure Kernel resolves this dilemma. It
enables an organization to prove specific claims
about its execution record without revealing the
record in full. The claim is proven. The sensitive
data remains private. Both conditions hold
simultaneously.

---

### Proving Claims Without Exposing Records

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: DISCLOSURE-KERNEL · HASH-CHAINING

The Disclosure Kernel uses Merkle inclusion proofs
to prove that a specific Evidence Object — or a
specific field within an Evidence Object — exists
within a batch without revealing the other records
in that batch.

The mechanics are straightforward. The batch is
merkleised — organized into a binary tree of hashes.
The Merkle root is published to the settlement layer.
To prove a specific claim, the Disclosure Kernel
provides:

- The specific Evidence Object or field being proven
- The path through the Merkle tree from that object
  to the root
- The published root hash on the settlement layer

A verifier can confirm that the specific object
is part of the batch by checking that the path
is valid and the root matches the published anchor.
The other records in the batch are not revealed.
The verifier learns only what they needed to know.

### The Multi-Party Pattern

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: DISCLOSURE-KERNEL · CVS-SIDECAR

The Disclosure Kernel enables multiple parties to
verify different claims about the same execution
record without sharing full records with each other.

A single CVS record may be relevant to multiple
parties simultaneously:
- A financial regulator verifying transaction limits
- An insurance underwriter verifying control activity
- A counterparty verifying settlement occurred
- An auditor verifying segregation of duties

Each party receives a scoped Merkle proof for their
specific claim. No party receives the full record.
No party can determine what other parties verified.
Each verification is independent.

This is not an access control model. It is a proof
model. The difference matters: access control gates
who can see data. The Disclosure Kernel proves claims
about data without revealing the data itself. A
verifier who receives a Merkle inclusion proof has
not been granted access to the evidence record.
They have received mathematical proof of a specific
claim about it.

### What the Disclosure Kernel Is Not

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: DISCLOSURE-KERNEL

The Disclosure Kernel is not:
- An access control system
- A role-based permissions layer
- A data filtering mechanism
- A privacy policy enforcement tool
- A system that gates access to evidence

Any implementation that treats the Disclosure Kernel
as access control — deciding who may see what —
rather than as proof generation — proving specific
claims without revealing others — is non-conformant.

The distinction is not semantic. Access control
requires trusting the system that implements it.
Proof generation requires trusting only the
mathematics. CVS evidence is valuable precisely
because it does not require trusting the operator.
A Disclosure Kernel implemented as access control
reintroduces operator trust through the back door.
