### The Public Sector Evidence Problem

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: TEMPORAL-INVERSION · TREASURY-GRAPH-MODEL

Government systems execute consequential actions
at scale — disbursing benefits, awarding contracts,
enforcing regulations, making administrative
decisions that affect millions of people. Each
of these actions requires democratic accountability:
citizens, courts, and oversight bodies must be
able to verify that government systems operated
within declared authority.

Current government IT architecture was not designed
for this accountability requirement. Systems generate
logs. Reports are assembled from logs. Oversight
bodies examine reports. The chain from action to
accountability runs through multiple layers of
reconstruction, each introducing uncertainty and
the possibility of selective presentation.

When AI systems begin acting with government authority
— as they already are in procurement, benefits
administration, regulatory enforcement, and workforce
management — this accountability gap becomes a
constitutional problem. The delegation chain that
democratic legitimacy requires must be verifiable.
CVS makes it verifiable.

### The Delegation Chain

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL · CVS-SIDECAR

Institutional authority works through delegation
chains. A law delegates power to an agency. The
agency delegates to a department. The department
delegates to an employee. At each link, the delegation
is documented, bounded, and auditable.

AI systems snap this chain. When a model ranks
employees for termination, it is exercising delegated
authority. When a chatbot routes a citizen's case
or updates a federal record, it is exercising delegated
authority. The question is whether that delegation
was valid at the exact moment the system acted —
and whether anyone can prove it.

CVS provides the administrative record that proves
the delegation chain was intact at execution time.
Not a report assembled after the fact. A cryptographic
record generated at the moment each delegated action
occurred, by a witness that cannot be instructed
by the system exercising the authority.

### The DOGE Case Study

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EXECUTION-BOUNDARY

The deployment of AI systems across federal agencies
by DOGE in 2025 demonstrated the governance gap
at civilisational scale.

AI systems were deployed to monitor employee
communications, generate ranked termination lists,
and route administrative decisions — all without
published, verifiable records of what each system
was authorized to do at the moment it acted.

Documented failures included termination of essential
workers managing bird flu outbreaks and nuclear
security — not software bugs, but authorization
failures: systems acting within their technical
permissions while operating outside any verifiable
governance boundary.

The evidence record was whatever the deploying
organization generated about itself. No independent
witness. No cryptographic proof of authorization.
No administrative record that predated the disputes.

CVS applied to DOGE-style deployments would have
produced: a cryptographic record of every action,
every authorization decision, every constraint
evaluation, anchored publicly, unalterable by the
system it describes. The administrative record
would have existed before the disputes, not been
assembled during them.

### Procurement and Benefits

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

Federal procurement and benefits administration
are the two largest fraud surfaces in government.
Both share the same structural weakness: execution
precedes validation, validation is interpretive,
and interpretation is slow.

CVS applied to procurement: every bid cryptographically
timestamped at submission, cost variance thresholds
declared at contract award as execution constraints,
change orders evaluated against declared parameters
before settlement.

CVS applied to benefits: attendance and eligibility
events recorded at execution time, payment gated on
cryptographic existence of verified records, ghost
beneficiary fraud mechanically impossible because
the proof requirement cannot be satisfied without
real participation.

In both cases, fraud does not become harder to execute.
It becomes structurally impossible. The attack surface
is removed, not policed.
