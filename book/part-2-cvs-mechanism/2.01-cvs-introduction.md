### The Problem CVS Solves

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

512 defines what must be true at the execution boundary.
But knowing what must be true is not the same as being
able to prove it was true.

A system can claim it enforced the seven invariants.
It can generate logs showing what it did. It can
produce reports asserting compliance. None of these
constitute independent proof. A system that generates
its own compliance evidence is the witness to its
own behavior — and a witness with a stake in the
outcome is not an independent witness.

> "A system cannot be the authoritative witness to
> its own failure."
> — Jonathan M. Watson

CVS solves this. It is an independent witness — a
system that observes execution from outside it,
records what occurred, and produces evidence that
cannot be altered by the system it describes.

512 is the constraint. CVS is the proof that the
constraint operated correctly. They are complementary
and independent.

---

### What CVS Is

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR

The Cryptographic Verification Sidecar is an
independent, fail-open evidence architecture.

It sits alongside a running system — never inside it —
and records cryptographic evidence of what occurred,
when it occurred, and under what declared constraints.

Four things CVS does not do:
- Block execution
- Interpret intent
- Decide outcomes
- Enforce policy

One thing CVS does:
- Witnesses

That is its complete function. A sidecar that does
anything more than witness is not a CVS. It is
something else — a policy engine, a control system,
a monitoring platform — and its evidence is not
independent.

### Why Sidecar Architecture

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: GEOMETRIC-DISJOINT · CVS-SIDECAR

The sidecar architecture is not a design preference.
It is the only architecture that produces independent
evidence.

Evidence is independent when it is produced by a
system that had no ability to influence the events
it recorded. The moment an evidence system can
influence execution — by blocking, by modifying,
by approving or denying — its evidence is no longer
independent. It is the record of a participant, not
a witness.

The sidecar sits alongside execution. It subscribes
to event streams the execution system already emits.
It never enters the execution path. It never
intercepts. It never participates.

This geometric separation — execution plane and
evidence plane running in parallel, never intersecting —
is what makes CVS evidence court-defensible,
insurance-priceable, and regulator-acceptable.

### Receipts Not Logs

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: EVIDENCE-OBJECT · CVS-SIDECAR

CVS produces receipts. Not logs.

A log records what a system did. Logs are generated
inside the system being described. They can be
altered, selectively preserved, or simply not generated
for events that the system's operators prefer not to
document. Logs explain behavior. They do not prove
authorization.

A receipt proves that a declared event occurred under
declared constraints at a specific moment in time.
Receipts are generated outside the system being
described. They are cryptographically sealed at
the moment of generation. They cannot be altered
without detection.

> "Logs explain behavior. Evidence proves authorization."
> — Jonathan M. Watson

This distinction — between a record that describes
and a receipt that proves — is the architectural
foundation of CVS. Everything else follows from it.

### Fail-Open by Design

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: FAIL-OPEN-POSTURE · CVS-SIDECAR

CVS is fail-open by design. If the sidecar is
temporarily unavailable, execution continues
uninterrupted. Evidence capture resumes when
connectivity returns. The gap is visible — a
discontinuity in the cryptographic chain — but
it does not halt the system.

This is not a compromise. It is a requirement.

A CVS implementation that blocks execution when
its evidence layer is offline has created a
dependency that violates the voluntary interaction
principles of 512. Exit must always be possible.
Execution cannot be held hostage by an evidence
system.

The fail-open design also means that gaps in the
evidence record are observable. An unobservable
failure is a defect. A visible gap is acceptable —
and informative. A gap in the CVS record is itself
evidence that something interrupted normal operation
at that moment.
