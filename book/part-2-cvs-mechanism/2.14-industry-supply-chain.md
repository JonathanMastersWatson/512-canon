### The Supply Chain Evidence Problem

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: EVIDENCE-OBJECT · TEMPORAL-INVERSION

Global supply chains are among the most complex
coordination systems humans have built. Goods move
across dozens of jurisdictions, through hundreds
of hands, under multiple legal frameworks, with
custody transferring at each step.

The evidence problem is structural. At every custody
transfer, the question is the same: did this
handoff occur under declared conditions, at the
declared time, with the declared goods? Yet the
evidence for each handoff is generated by the
parties to that handoff — who have incentives to
present the most favorable account of what occurred.

When disputes arise — and in global supply chains,
disputes are constant — the resolution process
requires reconstructing custody chains from
documents generated by interested parties, across
jurisdictions with different legal standards, often
months after the events in question.

CVS replaces reconstruction with replay.

### Custody Chain as Evidence Chain

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

Every custody transfer in a supply chain is an
execution event. Goods change hands. Documents
are signed. Financial instruments are triggered.
Each of these events has a precise moment, declared
conditions, and a binary outcome — the transfer
occurred under those conditions or it did not.

CVS treats each custody transfer as an execution
boundary event. At the moment of transfer, CVS
records:
- What was transferred
- Under what declared conditions
- By whom and to whom
- The cryptographic hash of the governing documents
- The timestamp

The record is generated at transfer time, by a
witness that has no stake in the outcome. It cannot
be altered retroactively. It does not depend on
the cooperation of either party to the transfer.

The custody chain becomes an evidence chain. Every
link is independently verifiable. No link can be
silently modified without breaking the chain.

### Trade Finance and Letters of Credit

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EXECUTION-BOUNDARY

Trade finance instruments — letters of credit, bills
of lading, documentary collections — exist because
supply chain counterparties do not trust each other.
The financial instruments substitute institutional
trust for direct counterparty trust.

The cost of this substitution is enormous. Letters
of credit require banks, lawyers, and weeks of
processing. Bills of lading disputes tie up cargo
and capital for months. The $6.8 trillion in supply
chain friction identified in the $50T calculation
is largely the cost of institutional trust substitution.

CVS reduces this cost by making direct evidence
available at the custody boundary. When both parties
to a transfer have access to a CVS record proving
exactly what occurred, the need for institutional
intermediation decreases. Trust shifts from
institutions to evidence. Evidence is cheaper,
faster, and more certain than institutional processes.

### Counterfeit and Provenance

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

Counterfeit goods cost the global economy over
$500 billion annually. The counterfeit problem
is fundamentally a provenance problem — the
inability to prove that a specific item traveled
the specific path it is claimed to have traveled.

CVS applied to supply chain creates a provenance
record that cannot be fabricated. Each custody
transfer adds a link to the evidence chain. The
complete chain from origin to destination is
independently verifiable. An item that cannot
produce a valid evidence chain either did not
travel the claimed path or the evidence was
not captured — either of which is informative.

This is not perfect counterfeit prevention.
It is structural counterfeit detection. Items
with valid evidence chains are provably genuine.
Items without valid evidence chains are suspect
by construction.
