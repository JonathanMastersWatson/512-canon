### MEMORANDUM

Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL · FIVE-RESPONSES-OF-POWER

**TO:** Secretary of State

**FROM:** [Implementing Official]

**RE:** Execution-Time AI Governance as Foreign

**DATE:** [Implementation Date]

---

**ISSUE**

The global competition for AI governance architecture
is a foreign policy competition. The nation whose
AI governance architecture becomes the global default
gains structural influence over every AI system
deployed internationally — how they are authorized,
what constraints they enforce, what evidence they
produce, and who can verify their behavior.

This competition is underway. It is not primarily
a military or economic competition. It is a standards
and infrastructure competition. The outcome will
be determined by which governance architecture
is adopted first at sufficient scale to become
the coordination default.

**THE TWO MODELS**

Two fundamentally different AI governance
architectures are competing for global adoption:

**Centralized interpretive governance** — AI systems
are governed by centralized authorities that review,
approve, and audit behavior after execution.
Authority flows from the center. Compliance is
demonstrated through periodic reporting to central
bodies. This model is favored by governments that
prefer centralized control — including the EU's
regulatory approach and, more extremely, authoritarian
models that use AI governance as a surveillance
and control infrastructure.

**Execution-time constraint verification** — AI
systems are governed by declared constraints
evaluated at the execution boundary. Authority
is distributed to the execution point. Compliance
is demonstrated by execution-time cryptographic
proof verifiable by any party. This model is
consistent with democratic principles of
transparent rules, equal enforcement, and
voluntary interaction.

These are not equivalent architectures with
different efficiency profiles. They embed
fundamentally different theories of legitimate
authority. The centralized model embeds the
assumption that authority flows from central
approval. The execution-time model embeds the
assumption that authority derives from declared
constraints applied transparently.

**GEOPOLITICAL IMPLICATIONS**

The nation whose governance architecture becomes
the global default shapes the answer to this
question for every AI system deployed globally:
where does authority reside?

If centralized interpretive governance becomes
the global default, authority resides in whoever
controls the central approval bodies. This
advantages nations with strong central institutions
and disadvantages nations whose populations value
distributed authority and individual sovereignty.

If execution-time constraint verification becomes
the global default, authority resides at the
execution boundary — distributed, transparent,
and verifiable by any party. This is structurally
consistent with democratic values and structurally
incompatible with covert surveillance architectures.

**PROPOSED ACTION**

1. Establish execution-time AI governance as a
   US foreign policy priority in multilateral
   AI governance forums — G7, G20, OECD, and
   bilateral technology agreements.

2. Incorporate execution-time constraint verification
   standards into US technology export frameworks —
   making US AI governance standards a condition
   of access to US AI technology and markets.

3. Develop bilateral AI governance agreements
   with allied nations based on execution-time
   constraint verification standards, creating
   an allied governance architecture that operates
   as a counterweight to centralized models.

4. Provide technical assistance to developing
   nations establishing AI governance frameworks,
   ensuring that US-aligned execution-time
   standards are available as an alternative
   to centralized models offered by authoritarian
   competitors.

**THE WINDOW**

The window for establishing execution-time
constraint verification as the global governance
default is measured in months, not years. Once
centralized interpretive governance frameworks
are embedded in international standards bodies
and bilateral agreements, displacing them requires
overcoming institutional inertia that grows
with every adoption.

The EU AI Act implementation is the immediate
competitive benchmark. Its provisions are being
translated into technical standards by ISO/IEC
committees now. If US execution-time standards
are not in those committees now, EU interpretive
standards will become the baseline.

**RECOMMENDATION**

Elevate execution-time AI governance to a foreign
policy priority. Engage multilateral forums
immediately. The competition is not for AI
capability. It is for AI governance architecture.
That competition is being lost by default.

### The Deeper Stakes

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL · OSSIFICATION

The geopolitical stakes extend beyond trade and
standards competition. AI governance architecture
determines whether AI systems deployed globally
are structurally transparent or structurally
opaque — whether their behavior can be verified
by the parties subject to them, or only by the
authorities that approved them.

Authoritarian AI governance is not merely inefficient.
It is a surveillance infrastructure that operates
at machine speed, at scale, without the visibility
gaps that human-speed surveillance creates.
Execution-time constraint verification is
structurally incompatible with covert surveillance
because it requires rules to be declared, visible,
and equally enforceable by all parties.

The choice between these governance architectures
is a choice about the kind of world that machine-speed
AI inhabits. That choice is being made now, in
standards bodies and bilateral agreements, largely
without public visibility.
