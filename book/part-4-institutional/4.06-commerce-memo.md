### MEMORANDUM

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL · CVS-SIDECAR

**TO:** Secretary of Commerce / Director, NIST

**FROM:** [Implementing Official]

**RE:** Execution-Time Constraint Verification —
Standards Development and International Coordination

**DATE:** [Implementation Date]

---

**ISSUE**

The United States currently lacks a published
federal standard for execution-time constraint
verification in AI systems. This gap creates three
compounding risks:

1. Foreign standards bodies — particularly the
   EU AI Act implementation framework and ISO/IEC
   technical committees — are developing AI governance
   standards that may not reflect US architectural
   preferences or interests.

2. US industry deploying AI systems internationally
   must comply with multiple incompatible standards
   frameworks, creating compliance overhead that
   disadvantages US companies relative to competitors
   operating under unified standards.

3. Without a published US standard, federal procurement
   requirements for AI system evidence cannot be
   specified precisely, creating ambiguity that
   delays adoption and creates legal uncertainty.

**BACKGROUND**

Execution-time constraint verification is the
technical capability by which an AI system proves,
at the moment it acts, that its action was authorized
under declared constraints. This capability is to
AI governance what cryptographic protocols are to
network security — a technical foundation that
enables trust without requiring trust in any
specific party.

NIST has existing authority and expertise to develop
standards in this domain through its AI Risk
Management Framework and its work on cryptographic
standards. The development of execution-time
constraint verification standards is a natural
extension of NIST's existing AI governance work.

**PROPOSED ACTION**

1. Direct NIST to develop a federal standard for
   execution-time constraint verification in AI
   systems, building on existing work in the AI
   Risk Management Framework and cryptographic
   standards.

2. Establish a technical working group including
   NIST, NSF, DARPA, and relevant industry partners
   to develop reference implementations and
   conformance testing frameworks.

3. Engage ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42 (Artificial Intelligence)
   to ensure US standards positions are represented
   in international AI governance standards development.

4. Incorporate execution-time constraint verification
   standards into federal AI procurement requirements
   within 24 months of standard publication.

**TRADE AND COMPETITIVENESS IMPLICATIONS**

Standards leadership in execution-time AI governance
creates durable US competitive advantage. Nations
whose AI systems conform to US-originated standards
are effectively operating within a US-designed
governance architecture. This is the same dynamic
by which US cryptographic standards — AES, SHA,
RSA — became global defaults that embed US
architectural preferences in international
infrastructure.

The window for US standards leadership in execution-time
AI governance is open now. The EU AI Act implementation
is still being developed. ISO standards are in early
stages. A US federal standard published within 18
months can establish the baseline that international
standards converge toward.

If the US does not publish standards, the EU will.
EU standards will reflect EU architectural preferences
— which emphasize centralized oversight and
interpretive compliance rather than execution-time
deterministic verification. US companies operating
internationally will be forced to comply with
EU-designed standards in EU markets and US-designed
standards in US markets, with no interoperability.

**RECOMMENDATION**

Direct NIST to initiate standards development
immediately. Establish 18-month publication target.
Engage international standards bodies concurrently.
Incorporate resulting standards into federal AI
procurement requirements on publication.

### Why Commerce

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL

Commerce is the correct institutional home for
this standards work because the competitive
implications are as significant as the governance
implications.

Execution-time AI governance standards are not
purely a safety or compliance matter. They are
a technology trade matter. The nation whose
architectural preferences are embedded in global
AI governance standards gains structural influence
over every AI system deployed internationally.

This is not a novel observation. The US has used
standards leadership as a trade and foreign policy
tool in every major technology transition. The
opportunity in AI governance is the same pattern,
applied at greater scale.
