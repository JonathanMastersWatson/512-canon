### MEMORANDUM

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: 512-KERNEL · CVS-SIDECAR

**TO:** Attorney General of the United States

**FROM:** [Implementing Official]

**RE:** Execution-Time Evidence Standards for AI
System Enforcement Actions — Guidance Request

**DATE:** [Implementation Date]

---

**ISSUE**

Federal enforcement actions involving AI systems
increasingly depend on evidence assembled after
execution from logs generated by the system under
scrutiny. This evidence is structurally vulnerable
to challenge: it is mutable, selectively preserved,
and produced by the party whose behavior is under
examination.

As AI systems take on greater operational authority —
executing decisions with legal consequences at
machine speed — the evidentiary standards governing
enforcement actions must evolve to reflect the
technical capacity for execution-time proof generation.

**BACKGROUND**

Current federal enforcement relies on system logs
as the primary evidence base for AI system behavior.
Logs have four structural weaknesses in enforcement
contexts:

1. Mutability — logs can be altered by parties
   with administrative access before enforcement
   proceedings begin.

2. Selectivity — log retention policies may result
   in evidence gaps that are indistinguishable from
   deliberate deletion.

3. Self-reporting — logs are generated by the system
   whose behavior is under examination. The system
   is the witness to its own conduct.

4. Reconstruction dependence — enforcement timelines
   require reconstructing what occurred from log
   fragments, introducing uncertainty that defendants
   exploit.

The technical capacity to replace reconstructed
log evidence with execution-time cryptographic
proof now exists. CVS-conforming implementations
produce Evidence Objects at the moment each action
occurs — by an independent witness, sealed
cryptographically, anchored to a public ledger,
and unalterable by any party including the operator.

**PROPOSED GUIDANCE**

The Department of Justice should issue guidance
establishing that:

1. In enforcement actions involving AI systems,
   execution-time cryptographic evidence produced
   by a conforming independent witness layer is
   presumptively admissible and carries greater
   evidentiary weight than reconstructed log evidence.

2. AI systems deployed in regulated industries
   that produce execution-time evidence conforming
   to published standards receive a rebuttable
   presumption of compliance with declared constraints
   for purposes of administrative enforcement.

3. AI systems that do not produce execution-time
   evidence are not thereby presumed non-compliant,
   but their operators bear the burden of producing
   equivalent evidence from other sources in
   enforcement proceedings.

**CRIMINAL ENFORCEMENT IMPLICATIONS**

Execution-time evidence transforms criminal
enforcement of AI-facilitated fraud. Current
prosecution of AI-facilitated fraud requires
reconstructing authorization chains from incomplete
records — a process that is expensive, time-consuming,
and often unsuccessful when entities have dissolved.

With execution-time CVS records:
- Authorization chains exist before prosecution begins
- Evidence cannot be altered or selectively preserved
- The administrative record predates the dispute
- Prosecution can proceed from proof not reconstruction

The $247 billion in annual improper payments
represents the largest enforcement opportunity
in federal history. Structural prevention through
execution-time proof is more efficient than
prosecution, but for cases where prosecution is
appropriate, CVS records provide the evidentiary
foundation that current log-based reconstruction
cannot.

**CIVIL ENFORCEMENT IMPLICATIONS**

In civil enforcement and regulatory proceedings,
execution-time evidence reduces discovery costs,
accelerates resolution, and narrows the scope
of contested facts. Organizations with CVS records
can demonstrate compliance specifically and
completely. Organizations without CVS records
cannot demonstrate the same level of compliance
specificity regardless of effort.

Over time, this evidentiary asymmetry becomes a
market signal: organizations that produce
execution-time evidence become preferred counterparties
in regulated industries.

**RECOMMENDATION**

Issue guidance establishing execution-time
cryptographic evidence as the preferred evidentiary
standard for AI system enforcement actions.
Convene a working group with NIST, OMB, and
relevant regulatory agencies to develop technical
standards for conforming implementations.
Publish standards within 180 days.

### Why This Memo Matters

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

Legal doctrine evolves through guidance, precedent,
and explicit standard-setting. The memo above
initiates the standard-setting process that will
eventually make execution-time evidence the default
evidentiary standard for AI system behavior.

When DOJ establishes this preference, every
organization deploying AI systems in regulated
industries has an evidentiary incentive to deploy
CVS — not because it is mandated, but because
organizations without CVS records are at evidentiary
disadvantage in every enforcement proceeding they
face.

The legal doctrine adoption curve follows the
insurance adoption curve with a 12–18 month lag.
When both converge, CVS deployment becomes the
path of least resistance for every AI-deploying
organization with regulatory exposure.
