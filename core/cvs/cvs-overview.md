## CRYPTOGRAPHIC VERIFICATION SIDECAR (CVS) — Overview

### Definition

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR

The Cryptographic Verification Sidecar (CVS) is an invented witness
architecture. It sits alongside a running system — never inside it —
and records cryptographic evidence of what occurred, when it occurred,
and under what declared constraints.

CVS does not block execution.
CVS does not interpret intent.
CVS does not decide outcomes.
CVS does not enforce policy.

It witnesses. That is its only job.

CVS produces receipts — not logs, not analytics, not behavioral
profiles. Each receipt is append-only, merkleised, and optionally
anchored to a public ledger for independent finality. The absence
of a receipt is itself observable — gaps cannot be hidden.

512 defines what must be true at the commit boundary.
CVS proves that it was true at the commit boundary.

512 is the constraint. CVS is the proof.

### Why Internal Logs Fail

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · GEOMETRIC-DISJOINT

Internal logs fail under adversarial scrutiny for one structural
reason: a system cannot be the authoritative witness to its own
failure.

> "A system cannot be the authoritative witness to its own failure."
> — Jonathan M. Watson

Internal logs are:
- Generated by the system under examination
- Subject to administrator access and modification
- Selectively preserved
- Dependent on the integrity of the system they describe

When disputes arise, when regulators examine behavior, when insurers
assess claims, when courts demand evidence — the question is not
what the system says it did. The question is what can be proven
independently.

CVS answers that question. Internal logs cannot.

### The Three-Plane Architecture

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · GEOMETRIC-DISJOINT

CVS operates across three planes that do not trust each other.
This separation is not incidental — it is the reason the system
is deployable, auditable, and insurable.

| Plane | Role | Trusts |
|---|---|---|
| Execution Plane | Primary system — performs actions | Nothing outside itself |
| Evidence Plane (CVS) | Witness — observes and records | Cryptographic proof only |
| Interpretation Plane | External — maps evidence to frameworks | Evidence plane output only |

The execution plane and evidence plane are geometrically disjoint.
They run in parallel, never intersecting. The sidecar observes
execution events out-of-band. It never intercepts, approves,
blocks, or participates in execution.

This separation is what makes CVS evidence independently verifiable.
A witness that participates in what it witnesses is not a witness.

### Core Properties

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · FAIL-OPEN-POSTURE

A CVS-conforming implementation satisfies five non-negotiable
properties:

**Fail-Open:** Evidence systems must never block, delay, or interrupt
execution. If CVS is offline, execution continues. Evidence capture
resumes when connectivity returns. A gap is observable — a missing
hash segment, a discontinuity in time ordering. An unobservable
failure is a defect. A visible gap is acceptable.

**Witness-Only:** The sidecar may observe and record, but never act,
decide, or enforce. The moment CVS participates in decisions, its
independence is compromised and its evidence becomes an argument
rather than a record.

**Authority Limits:** No hidden, delegated, or implicit power is
permitted. CVS has no control surface.

**Selective Disclosure:** Transparency must be precise, bounded, and
minimal. CVS records that an event occurred — not who the end user
was, what they asked, or how they behave over time.

**Independent Verification:** Verification must be possible without
trusting the operator. Any third party must be able to verify
evidence independently without privileged system access.

### Evidence Objects — Receipts Not Logs

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · EVIDENCE-OBJECT

CVS captures Evidence Objects — not logs. The distinction is
architecturally significant.

A log records what a system did.
A receipt proves that a declared event occurred under declared
constraints at a specific moment in time.

Every Evidence Object answers four questions:

1. What was proposed — intent hash
2. What rules were active — spec hash
3. What decision was made — per-invariant evaluation results
4. What occurred — execution outcome

Each Evidence Object is self-contained. It must be interpretable
without access to the system that produced it, without trusting
the operator, and without external context of any kind.

Evidence Objects are chained cryptographically. Each links to the
previous. Any attempt to alter, delete, or insert a record breaks
the chain. Gaps are detectable. Tampering is visible.

> "Logs explain behavior. Evidence proves authorization."
> — Jonathan M. Watson

### Settlement Layer

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR

CVS anchors Merkle roots to a neutral public settlement layer.
Only cryptographic fingerprints are published — no operational
data, no personal data, no content.

The anchor proves three things:
- The execution trace existed at a specific moment
- The sequence has not been altered since
- Any subsequent change is mathematically detectable

The XRP Ledger is the reference settlement layer for CVS because
it provides deterministic finality in 3–5 seconds, negligible
and predictable costs, and public verifiability without requiring
trust in any operator.

The architecture is ledger-agnostic by design. Any settlement
layer satisfying the mandatory properties may be substituted.

### What CVS Is Not

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR

CVS is not:
- A logging system
- A monitoring platform
- A compliance tool
- An access control system
- A policy engine
- An analytics pipeline
- A control system
- An alerting system
- A truth engine
- A correctness validator

CVS is a witness layer for execution-bound evidence. It records
what happened at the commit boundary, proves that record has not
been tampered with, and enables independent verification of
specific claims without full data exposure.

That is its complete function. Nothing more is claimed.

### Relationship to 512

Source: Jonathan M. Watson | 512 / CVS — Watson
Concept-ID: CVS-SIDECAR · 512-KERNEL

CVS is compatible with, but independent of, the 512 constraint set.

512 defines discovered constraints governing execution-time
legitimacy. CVS defines one witness architecture that can operate
alongside systems satisfying 512's properties.

Neither governs the other. CVS may operate without 512. Systems
satisfying 512's properties may use witness architectures other
than CVS.
